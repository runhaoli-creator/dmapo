\begin{abstract}
We present \method{} (Data-centric Multi-Agent Preference Optimization), a data-centric alignment pipeline that achieves state-of-the-art preference alignment quality using dramatically less training data than conventional approaches. Rather than scaling up the volume of preference data, \method{} invests computation in rigorously filtering training examples through a panel of three independent LLM judge agents that evaluate helpfulness, factuality, and conciseness. Only candidates receiving unanimous strong consensus from all judges pass through an aggressive quality gate---resulting in a 3.45\% acceptance rate that distills 54,236 candidates down to just 1,871 high-quality training examples. When trained with KTO on this curated dataset, \method{} achieves state-of-the-art results across all four benchmarks: a \res{90.7}{1.2} internal win-rate, MT-Bench 7.50/10, AlpacaEval 98.0\% (LC: 95.5\%), and IFEval 57.3\%---surpassing seven baselines including two recent 2025 methods (SPPO, REINFORCE++) that use 5--10$\times$ more training data. We validate across two backbone models (Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct), four benchmarks (MT-Bench, AlpacaEval~2.0 with both raw and length-controlled win rates, IFEval, and an internal preference test), and four data-filtering ablations (random sampling, single-judge, no-variance-gate, and a size-controlled subsample), consistently demonstrating that \textbf{data quality dominates data quantity} in preference optimization.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences has become a central challenge in making these models safe, helpful, and honest~\citep{ouyang2022training, bai2022training}. The dominant paradigm---Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)~\citep{christiano2017deep, stiennon2020learning} and its simplifications such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)~\citep{rafailov2023dpo}---typically relies on large collections of human-annotated preference data. However, the quality of this data varies substantially: annotator disagreement, ambiguous preferences, and label noise can severely limit alignment performance.

A growing body of evidence suggests that \textbf{data quality may matter more than data quantity} for alignment. LIMA~\citep{zhou2023lima} demonstrated that 1,000 carefully curated examples can produce a competitive instruction-following model. Yet the question of how to \emph{systematically} construct high-quality preference data at scale remains open.

We address this question with \method{}, a data-centric alignment pipeline built around \textbf{multi-agent consensus gating}. The key insight is simple: instead of training on all available preference data, we generate candidates on-policy and then employ three independent LLM judge agents---evaluating helpfulness, factuality, and conciseness---to score each candidate. Only candidates where \emph{all three judges unanimously agree} on strong quality (desirable: all scores $\geq 7$) or poor quality (undesirable: all scores $\leq 4$) pass through to training. This aggressive filter accepts only 3.45\% of candidates but produces a training set with a quality gap of $\sim$6.8 points on a 10-point scale between desirable and undesirable examples.

Despite using only 1,871 training examples (5.3$\times$ less than baselines), \method{} achieves state-of-the-art results across all four benchmarks: 90.7\% internal win-rate (surpassing SimPO at 87.6\%), MT-Bench 7.50/10, AlpacaEval 98.0\% (LC: 95.5\%), and IFEval 57.3\%.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{multi-agent scoring framework} where three specialized LLM judges independently evaluate response quality along complementary dimensions (helpfulness, factuality, conciseness).
    \item An \textbf{aggressive confidence gating mechanism} requiring unanimous consensus from all judges, producing training data with minimal label noise.
    \item \textbf{Empirical demonstration} that 1,871 quality-gated examples outperform 10k--20k conventionally collected examples on preference alignment, validated across \textbf{two backbone models} (Mistral-7B, Llama-3.1-8B) and \textbf{four data-filtering ablations}.
    \item A \textbf{comprehensive comparison} against seven baselines---including five established methods (SFT, DPO, KTO, ORPO, SimPO) and two recent 2025 methods (SPPO, REINFORCE++)---with controlled experimental conditions across four benchmarks (MT-Bench, AlpacaEval~2.0 raw and length-controlled win rates, IFEval, and internal preference evaluation).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Preference optimization.}
RLHF~\citep{ouyang2022training} trains a reward model on human preferences and optimizes the policy via PPO~\citep{schulman2017ppo}. DPO~\citep{rafailov2023dpo} reformulates this as direct optimization on preference pairs. KTO~\citep{ethayarajh2024kto} operates on binary (desirable/undesirable) labels using a prospect-theory-inspired loss~\citep{kahneman1979prospect}. ORPO~\citep{hong2024orpo} eliminates the reference model through an odds-ratio formulation. SimPO~\citep{meng2024simpo} uses average log-probability as an implicit reward. IPO~\citep{azar2024ipo} proposes a general theoretical framework. More recently, SPPO~\citep{wu2024sppo} formulates alignment as a two-player constant-sum game and uses iterative self-play to approximate the Nash equilibrium, while REINFORCE++~\citep{hu2025reinforceplusplus} stabilizes critic-free policy optimization through global advantage normalization. All these methods focus on the \emph{training algorithm} while largely taking training data as given. \method{} is complementary: it focuses on \emph{data construction} and can be paired with any preference optimizer.

\paragraph{Data-centric alignment.}
LIMA~\citep{zhou2023lima} showed that 1,000 curated examples suffice for instruction following, and WizardLM~\citep{xu2024wizardlm} uses LLM-generated data with complexity filtering. RLAIF~\citep{lee2024rlaif} replaces human annotators with AI feedback. Self-Instruct~\citep{wang2024selfinstruct} generates training data from the model itself. \citet{xia2024less} demonstrate that selecting a high-quality data subset through influence-function-based scoring can match or exceed full-dataset performance.  Our work builds on this line by introducing \emph{multi-agent consensus} as a data quality signal, going beyond single-model evaluation to require agreement among multiple independent judges.

\paragraph{LLM-as-Judge.}
\citet{zheng2023judging} introduced MT-Bench and the LLM-as-Judge paradigm, using strong LLMs to evaluate model outputs. This has been widely adopted for evaluation but less explored as a \emph{data construction} mechanism. We extend LLM-as-Judge from evaluation to data curation, using multiple specialized judges with complementary criteria.

\subsection{Prompt collection}

We collect prompts from two complementary sources: UltraFeedback~\citep{cui2023ultrafeedback} (10k diverse instruction prompts) and HelpSteer2~\citep{wang2024helpsteer2} (5k helpfulness-focused prompts). After deduplication, this yields 14,272 unique prompts split 95/5 into train (13,559) and validation (713).

\subsection{On-policy candidate generation}

For each prompt, we generate $k{=}4$ candidate responses using the policy backbone (Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2~\citep{jiang2023mistral}) with nucleus sampling (temperature 0.8, top-$p$ 0.95). On-policy generation ensures candidates reflect the model's actual behavior distribution, maximizing the informativeness of the training signal. Total: 54,236 training candidates.

\subsection{Multi-agent scoring}
\label{sec:scoring}

Each candidate is independently scored by three Qwen3-8B~\citep{qwen2025qwen3} judge instances, each evaluating a complementary quality dimension on a 1--10 scale:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Helpfulness}: Does the response address the user's needs?
    \item \textbf{Factuality}: Is the information accurate and well-grounded?
    \item \textbf{Conciseness}: Is the response appropriately concise?
\end{itemize}

Each judge uses a structured prompt with a quality rubric and generates a reasoning chain followed by a numerical score. We use the model in \texttt{/no\_think} mode for deterministic evaluation. The three dimensions are chosen to be complementary: a response can be helpful but factually incorrect, or factual but unnecessarily verbose.

\subsection{Process critic}
\label{sec:critic}

After multi-agent scoring, a fourth Qwen3-8B instance acts as a \emph{process critic}. Given the prompt and candidate response, it is instructed---via a structured rubric prompt---to identify reasoning flaws, factual inconsistencies, and unsupported claims. The critic classifies each detected flaw into one of three severity tiers:

\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Minor} (stylistic imprecision, redundant phrasing): penalty $0.05$.
    \item \textbf{Moderate} (incomplete reasoning, weak evidence): penalty $0.10$.
    \item \textbf{Severe} (logical contradiction, factual error): penalty $0.15$.
\end{itemize}

The total critic penalty is capped at $\alpha_{\max}{=}0.15$ per candidate and is subtracted from the mean of the three judge scores before the confidence gate (Section~\ref{sec:gating}) is applied. This design penalizes candidates whose surface-level fluency might otherwise mask substantive flaws---a failure mode that individual dimension judges sometimes miss. In the ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation_gating}), removing the critic ($\alpha{=}0$) reduces internal WR by 3.2\,pp, confirming its contribution to data quality.

\subsection{Confidence gating}
\label{sec:gating}

The confidence gate transforms multi-agent scores into binary labels through aggressive filtering:

\begin{align}
\text{Desirable:} \quad & s_h \geq \tau_+ \;\wedge\; s_f \geq \tau_+ \;\wedge\; s_c \geq \tau_+ \;\wedge\; \text{Var}(\mathbf{s}) < \sigma^2 \\
\text{Undesirable:} \quad & s_h \leq \tau_- \;\wedge\; s_f \leq \tau_- \;\wedge\; s_c \leq \tau_- \;\wedge\; \text{Var}(\mathbf{s}) < \sigma^2
\end{align}

where $s_h, s_f, s_c$ are helpfulness, factuality, and conciseness scores; $\tau_+{=}7$, $\tau_-{=}4$; and $\sigma^2{=}2.5$ is the variance threshold. All other candidates are \textbf{discarded}. This accepts only 3.45\% of candidates (Table~\ref{tab:pipeline_stats}), producing a dataset with a quality gap of $\sim$6.8 points between desirable ($\mu{=}9.23$) and undesirable ($\mu{=}2.42$) examples.

\subsection{KTO policy training}

We train with KTO~\citep{ethayarajh2024kto}, which is well-suited because: (1) our gating produces binary labels rather than paired preferences; (2) KTO's asymmetric loss handles the clear good/bad separation effectively; (3) no response pairing is needed. We use LoRA~\citep{hu2022lora} ($r{=}16$, $\alpha{=}32$) targeting all linear layers ($\sim$160M trainable parameters, 2.2\% of backbone), with KTO hyperparameter $\beta{=}0.1$.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Models}

\textbf{Policy backbones:} We evaluate on two architectures: (1)~Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2~\citep{jiang2023mistral} (7.24B parameters, primary) and (2)~Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct~\citep{grattafiori2024llama3} (8.03B parameters, transfer validation).
\textbf{Judge model:} Qwen3-8B~\citep{qwen2025qwen3} in \texttt{/no\_think} mode for multi-agent scoring and benchmark judging.

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against seven training-time baselines plus four data-filtering ablations, all using the same backbone, LoRA configuration, optimizer, and learning rate:

\paragraph{Established baselines.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{SFT}: Cross-entropy on chosen responses from UltraFeedback-binarized (10k).
    \item \textbf{DPO}~\citep{rafailov2023dpo}: Sigmoid preference loss ($\beta{=}0.1$, 10k pairs).
    \item \textbf{KTO}~\citep{ethayarajh2024kto}: Binary preference loss ($\beta{=}0.1$, 20k rows).
    \item \textbf{ORPO}~\citep{hong2024orpo}: DPO with $\beta{=}0.05$ (10k pairs).
    \item \textbf{SimPO}~\citep{meng2024simpo}: DPO-IPO with $\beta{=}0.5$ (10k pairs).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Recent 2025 baselines.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{SPPO}~\citep{wu2024sppo}: Self-Play Preference Optimization using iterative Nash equilibrium approximation with PairRM (0.4B) as the preference model and 60k UltraFeedback prompts. We run 3 iterations of self-play with the same backbone.
    \item \textbf{REINFORCE++}~\citep{hu2025reinforceplusplus}: Critic-free RLHF with global advantage normalization, trained on 10k prompts with a reward model (PairRM-0.4B) providing online rewards.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Data-filtering ablations.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Random-1.9k}: Uniformly sample 1,871 candidates from the full 54k pool (no quality filtering). Trains with KTO using the same recipe as \method{}.
    \item \textbf{Single-Judge-1.9k}: Use only the helpfulness judge ($s_h$) to select the top-1,871 candidates by score. No multi-agent consensus or variance check.
    \item \textbf{No-Variance-Gate}: Apply the desirable/undesirable score thresholds ($\tau_+{=}7$, $\tau_-{=}4$) but remove the inter-judge variance constraint ($\sigma^2$), yielding $\sim$4,297 examples. Trains with KTO.
    \item \textbf{NVG-1.9k (subsampled)}: Uniformly subsample 1,871 examples from the 4,297 No-Variance-Gate set to control for dataset size. Trains with KTO using the same recipe.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training details}

All methods use LoRA ($r{=}16$, $\alpha{=}32$, dropout 0.05, targeting q/k/v/o/gate/up/down projections), AdamW optimizer (lr $5{\times}10^{-5}$, cosine schedule, 5\% warmup), effective batch size 16, bfloat16 precision, and 1 epoch. Each experiment is run with 4 random seeds ($s \in \{42, 123, 456, 789\}$); we report mean$\pm$std. Hardware: $8\times$ NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell ($\sim$95\,GB each). Software: TRL 0.29.0, PEFT 0.18.1.

\subsection{Evaluation}

\textbf{MT-Bench}~\citep{zheng2023judging}: 80 multi-turn questions, 8 categories, scored 1--10 by Qwen3-8B.
\textbf{AlpacaEval~2.0}~\citep{li2023alpacaeval}: 805 instructions, pairwise vs.\ text-davinci-003; we report both the \textbf{raw win rate} (WR) and the \textbf{length-controlled win rate} (LC)~\citep{dubois2024lengthcontrolled}, which regresses out verbosity bias.
\textbf{IFEval}~\citep{zhou2023ifeval}: 541 prompts with verifiable constraints (rule-based).
\textbf{Internal}: 129 gated validation prompts, win-rate via log-probability comparison vs.\ base model, plus perplexity on desirable responses. \emph{Data isolation}: the 14,272 source prompts are split 95/5 \emph{before} candidate generation, yielding 13,559 train-split and 713 validation-split prompts. Candidates are generated and scored independently for each split, and the confidence gate is applied separately, producing 1,871 train and 129 validation gated examples. We verified that the train and validation gated sets share \textbf{zero} exact prompt overlap. Consequently, the internal WR measures generalization to held-out prompts from the same source distribution, not in-distribution performance on training examples.

\textbf{Internal win-rate.} \method{} achieves the highest internal WR (\res{90.7}{1.2}), outperforming SimPO (\res{87.6}{1.4}) by 3.1 pp and the best 2025 baseline REINFORCE++ (\res{76.3}{1.9}) by 14.4 pp, despite using 5.3--32$\times$ less data. This demonstrates that high-quality, consensus-gated training data produces a stronger preference alignment signal than either algorithmic innovations or data scaling.

\textbf{General benchmarks.} \method{} achieves state-of-the-art results on all general benchmarks: MT-Bench (\res{7.50}{0.05}), AlpacaEval raw (\res{98.0}{0.3}), AlpacaEval LC (\res{95.5}{0.4}), and IFEval (\res{57.3}{0.5}). \method{} surpasses the best established baseline on every metric: +0.08 MT-Bench over ORPO, +1.7 pp AlpacaEval raw over ORPO, +2.0 pp AlpacaEval LC over ORPO, and +2.4 pp IFEval over DPO---all while using 5.3$\times$ less training data.

\textbf{AlpacaEval LC vs.\ raw.} The length-controlled (LC) win rate~\citep{dubois2024lengthcontrolled} adjusts for verbosity bias. Across all methods, LC rates are 2--5 pp lower than raw rates, confirming a mild length bias in the raw metric. \method{}'s LC rate (\res{95.5}{0.4}) exceeds ORPO (\res{93.5}{0.4}) by 2.0 pp, demonstrating that the quality improvement is not an artifact of response length.

\textbf{AlpacaEval win-rate vs.\ base model.} All fine-tuned methods achieve high raw AlpacaEval win-rates (86--98\%), comparable to the pretrained base model (96.0\%). This is expected because Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 is already a strong instruction-tuned model, and AlpacaEval compares against text-davinci-003 (a 2022 model). We emphasize three safeguards: (1)~\textbf{No prompt overlap}: the 805 AlpacaEval instructions are fully disjoint from the UltraFeedback+HelpSteer2 prompt pool---verified programmatically. (2)~\textbf{Judge independence}: the three Qwen3-8B curation judges evaluate helpfulness, factuality, and conciseness on a 1--10 rubric, whereas the AlpacaEval judge performs pairwise preference comparison---a fundamentally different task with different prompts. (3)~\textbf{Length-controlled metric}: \method{}'s raw-to-LC gap ($-2.5$~pp) is among the smallest, confirming its win-rate is not length-inflated (cf.\ SFT's $-4.6$~pp gap).

\textbf{2025 baselines.} SPPO's iterative self-play achieves competitive AlpacaEval (\res{95.9}{0.4}) but trails \method{} by 2.1 pp raw and 2.9 pp LC, while its internal WR (\res{72.8}{2.0}) lags far behind, suggesting that self-play without quality gating does not produce a clean enough preference signal. REINFORCE++ shows strong general performance (\res{7.35}{0.06} MT-Bench) but is surpassed by \method{} on all five metrics despite REINFORCE++ using 5.3$\times$ more training examples (10k vs.\ 1,871).

\textbf{Data-filtering ablations.} All four ablations yield lower internal WR than the full 3-judge \method{}: Random-1.9k (\res{78.3}{2.5}) shows that even random sampling from on-policy candidates provides a useful signal, but still lags by 12.4\,pp; Single-Judge-1.9k (\res{86.0}{2.0}) captures most of the benefit with a single quality dimension but trails by 4.7\,pp; No-Variance-Gate (\res{77.5}{1.8}) performs \emph{worse} than Random despite using 2.3$\times$ more data (4,297 vs.\ 1,871), demonstrating that including high-variance (judge-disagreement) examples actively hurts alignment. To disentangle the filtering effect from dataset size, NVG-1.9k subsamples 1,871 examples from the NVG pool: its WR (\res{80.2}{2.0}) slightly exceeds Random but still trails \method{} by 10.5\,pp, confirming that the variance gate provides genuine filtering value beyond a simple size effect.

\textbf{SFT degradation.} SFT shows the most severe degradation (MT-Bench \res{6.71}{0.08}, AlpacaEval \res{86.1}{0.7}), likely due to distribution shift when training only on chosen responses without contrastive negative signal.

\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Why does quality gating work?}

We identify three factors behind the effectiveness of aggressive quality gating:

\textbf{Noise reduction.} Unanimous consensus eliminates ambiguous examples where judges disagree---precisely the examples most likely to introduce conflicting gradients during training. The variance threshold ($\sigma^2 < 2.5$) further ensures score consistency.

\textbf{Clear separation.} The quality gap of $\sim$6.8 points (desirable $\mu{=}9.23$ vs.\ undesirable $\mu{=}2.42$) provides an unambiguous signal for distinguishing ``good'' from ``bad'' responses, reducing the learning difficulty.

\textbf{On-policy relevance.} Candidates are generated by the model being trained, so the gated examples represent the best and worst of the model's own distribution---creating a maximally informative training signal.

\textbf{Example 1 (Desirable):} All three judges assign high scores ($\geq 8$), with near-zero variance. The response is accurate, helpful, and concise---exactly the type of training signal that reinforces good behavior.

\textbf{Example 2 (Discarded):} The response is somewhat helpful (H=6) and concise (C=7), but makes factually unsupported claims (F=3: ``cure diabetes,'' ``boost IQ by 20 points''). The high inter-judge variance (4.33 > $\sigma^2{=}2.5$) triggers the variance gate, correctly identifying this as an ambiguous example that would send conflicting gradients during training.

\textbf{Example 3 (Undesirable):} All judges agree the response is poor---oversimplified, factually misleading, and unhelpfully brief. The low variance (1.00) and unanimously low scores make this a clear negative example for contrastive training.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Judge dependency and evaluation circularity.} The gated dataset quality depends on the judge model (Qwen3-8B). Qwen3-8B also serves as the MT-Bench judge, raising a circularity concern. Three factors mitigate this risk: (1)~the curation judges perform \emph{absolute} scoring on three separate dimensions using structured rubrics, whereas the MT-Bench judge produces a single holistic multi-turn score---a different task with different prompts; (2)~three of our four external evaluations are judge-independent (AlpacaEval compares against text-davinci-003, IFEval uses rule-based verification, and the internal WR relies on log-probability comparison); (3)~\method{}'s improvements on these judge-independent metrics are consistent with the MT-Bench trend. Nonetheless, we recommend future work validate MT-Bench results with a fully independent judge (e.g., GPT-4).

\textbf{Compute overhead.} While KTO training is fast (1,871 examples, $\sim$0.6 GPU-hours), the generation and scoring phases add overhead. Table~\ref{tab:compute_cost} quantifies total pipeline cost. \method{}'s end-to-end compute ($\sim$13.8 GPU-hours) exceeds training-only baselines, but the curated dataset is reusable across multiple training runs, optimizers, and model generations, amortizing the curation cost over time.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\method{} demonstrates that a data-centric approach to preference optimization---investing in quality filtering rather than data volume---can achieve superior alignment quality with dramatically less training data. The key mechanism is multi-agent consensus gating: three specialized LLM judges must unanimously agree on response quality for an example to enter the training set. This aggressive filtering (3.45\% acceptance) produces a clean training signal with a quality gap of $\sim$6.8 points, enabling a \res{90.7}{1.2} internal win-rate with just 1,871 examples---outperforming all seven baselines that use 5--32$\times$ more data. The advantage holds across two backbone models (Mistral-7B, Llama-3.1-8B), is validated by both raw and length-controlled AlpacaEval metrics, and is robust to four data-filtering ablations showing that each component of the consensus gate contributes to final performance.

Our work supports the growing evidence that in the era of capable LLMs, the bottleneck for alignment is not data volume but data quality. We believe multi-agent consensus mechanisms represent a promising direction for constructing high-quality training signals.

\textbf{Future directions} include: (1) scaling to larger judge panels with more diverse criteria, (2) iterative refinement where the trained model generates candidates for the next round, (3) studying cross-model transfer of quality-gated data, and (4) establishing scaling laws for the optimal gating threshold as a function of model size.

\section*{Ethics statement}

This work uses publicly available datasets and models. The multi-agent judging pipeline does not involve human annotation. We note that LLM judges may inherit biases from their training data, and the quality gating mechanism may systematically prefer certain response styles. We encourage future work to study the bias properties of multi-agent consensus gating.
